About Jonathan

The timeline from Air Force to Implementation PM to product builder. Why this transition makes sense and what I bring that most APM candidates don't.

The story

Leaving the Air Force was harder than I expected. Not the work part — the people part.

Seven years in Security Forces meant being surrounded by people from every background imaginable — and learning how to work with all of them under pressure. When that ended, the transition to corporate life was quieter than I was ready for. More isolated. I knew how to execute, but I didn't yet know where I fit.

Implementation PM work changed that. It gave me back what I missed most — real problems, real people, and the kind of collaboration where your ability to read a room and build trust actually determines the outcome. Every client is different. Every implementation has a moment where the plan meets reality and you have to think fast.

Three years of that work put me in a specific seat: the person who sees what the product promised and what the client actually got. I watched well-intentioned features create months of cleanup. I watched small, well-scoped fixes save teams weeks of work, every week. That's the perspective I'm bringing to product — I've seen what happens after the feature ships.

Outside of work I'm in the middle of my first running journey — several 5Ks and 10Ks in, with a half marathon on the calendar before the year is out. The goal keeps moving forward. That feels right.

What I bring

Why this transition makes sense.

👁️
Downstream awareness
I know what a feature costs after launch: support tickets, client confusion, rework. Most new PMs learn this after their first painful launch. I've already learned it.
🛠️
Builder's instinct
I didn't wait for a roadmap slot to fix the broken intake process. I scoped the smallest version of a solution that would work and shipped it. That's the instinct I want to keep using.
🤝
Cross-functional fluency
Engineering, client stakeholders, executive sponsors — I've translated between all three on every project I've run. That's the job of a PM.
⚖️
Tradeoff thinking
Every implementation is a series of scope decisions under time pressure. I'm comfortable saying what we're not building and why. Saying no is part of the job.
📊
Outcome orientation
I think in terms of what changed because of a decision, not just whether the task was completed. The metric that matters is the one that reflects the user's experience.
🧭
Domain depth
Enterprise SaaS, higher education, ERP integration. I understand the client world my products will serve better than most candidates who haven't lived in it.
