02 / The product story

How I thought about it
as a product problem.

01
The problem I noticed
Every implementation started with a static Word document that clients filled out before kickoff. It didn't validate answers, didn't adapt based on what a client told us, and produced inconsistent data. PMs spent the first week of every project chasing missing information. That repetition across every single project is what made it feel like a real product problem worth solving – not just a process inconvenience.
02
How I scoped the fix
I didn't start with code. I mapped every BRQ field against how often it required follow-up clarification. High-failure fields needed better prompts. Fields irrelevant to certain client types needed to become conditional. The decision: build a guided, conditional flow that solves 80% of the problem in v1 rather than waiting to solve all of it perfectly. Shipped faster, learned faster.
03
What I intentionally left out of v1
Autosave: adds state management complexity and privacy considerations – deferred until usage data showed meaningful abandonment. A client-facing portal: out of scope for v1, this was an internal PM tool. Progress saving across browser sessions: deferred pending data on where clients actually drop off. Every item left out of v1 was a deliberate decision, not an oversight.
04
What I'd do differently with a full product team
Usage analytics from day one: which fields cause the most rework, where users drop off, how long completion actually takes. Right now I know it works because PMs tell me so. With a real product team I'd want data to confirm it, and I'd use that data to drive v2 prioritization. I'd also want a formal feedback loop with clients themselves – they're the ones filling it out.
03 / What I learned

Product instincts this project built.

🔍
Start with the workaround
PMs were sending 4-5 follow-up emails per client. That workaround was the product problem. The best place to look for what to build is what people are already doing to compensate for what doesn't exist.
✂️
Scope is a product decision
Every feature I left out of v1 was a deliberate tradeoff – not a gap. Knowing what not to build and being able to explain why is as important as knowing what to build. Ship the 80% solution first.
📊
Adoption is a product metric
Leadership adopting the tool for the whole team wasn't a compliment – it was the KPI. A tool nobody uses isn't a product. The measure of success was team-wide rollout, not just "it worked for me."
What this says about how I think
I didn't wait for permission or a roadmap slot. I saw a problem costing real time across every project, every week, scoped the smallest version of a fix that would actually work, and shipped it. That's the instinct I want to keep using – with the title, the team, and the resources of an actual product role.
