02 / Behind the build

How I thought about it
as a product problem.

01
The problem I noticed
Every implementation started with a static Word document that clients filled out before kickoff. It didn't validate answers, didn't adapt based on what a client told us, and produced inconsistent data. PMs spent the first week of every project chasing missing information. That repetition across every project is what made it feel like a real product problem worth solving.
02
How I scoped the fix
I didn't start with code. I mapped every BRQ field against how often it required follow-up clarification. High-failure fields needed better prompts. Irrelevant fields for certain client types needed to become conditional. The decision: build a guided, conditional intake flow that solves 80% of the problem in v1, rather than waiting to solve all of it perfectly.
03
What I intentionally left out of v1
Autosave: adds state management complexity and privacy considerations. Skip it, ship a shorter flow first. Client-facing portal: out of scope for v1, this was an internal PM tool. Progress saving across sessions: deferred pending usage data on where clients actually drop off.
04
What I'd do differently with a product team
Usage analytics from day one: which fields cause the most rework, where users drop off, how long completion actually takes. Right now I know it works because PMs tell me so. With a real product team I'd want the data to confirm it, and I'd use that data to drive v2 prioritization.
What this says about how I think
I didn't wait for permission or a roadmap slot. I saw a problem costing real time across every project, scoped the smallest version of a fix that would actually work, and shipped it. That's the muscle I want to keep using — just with the resources and title of an actual product role.
